From a preliminary check, it seems ORB is more reliable for key point tracking in this application based off the test imagery of checkered boards and small background objects which will be relevant in the experiments used for the VO pipeline (camera being moved around my room or library with checkered pattern in frame at the start or end and small background objects). This is qualitatively evident in ORB_test1 versus SIFT_test1 when comparing how many keypoints were assigned to the checkerboard versus a fairly small background object of a bottle cap and how many points were accurately tracked across orientation. SIFT_test1 assigns way more key points around the stationary bottle cap (instead of distributing them across objects more evenly) and way less to what would be a desirable to be a main focus, the checkered pattern being moved, while still fairly inaccurately tracking th points it did assign to it. For this reason, ORB will be used going forward.

K-cluster and DBscan were tested in tandem with RANSAC for filtering important keypoints. K-clusters has an unclear tuning process, with certain cluster counts doing better than others, but not in an increasing fashion but a random alternating pattern. In general, DBscan seems to be more efficient since kclusters demands a higher count around 12 at least and with ease selects matches that are more accurate (at worst 2 squares off with most 1 or less) while k-clusters typically is 4-5 squares off. This is likely thanks to the epsilon parameter which allows DBscan to eliminate clusters with points too far from one another, while kclusters compacts points together based on how many clusters are expected to be taken from the total. Therefore, with kclusters it's less straight forward and computationally expensive (b/c more clusters are required) to compact the points together and filter out squares with inaccurately spread out matches). For now, DBscan will be moved forward.

Baseline essential matrix function from cv2 is somewhat accurate with projection across runs. However, still far below acceptable for straight trajectory for trail_walk.mp4, and goes different directions between runs. Also, sometimes switches 180 in trajectory. These issues are likely due to numerical drift, inherent degeneracies due to a single plane/monocular perspective, well as the keypoints tracked significantly, almost sporatically varying across frames. 

At this point, Epipolar geometry code approach for building poses fails completely due to solving issues and there remains issues with the classical approach (triangulation isn't succeeding, but this will be replaced with readings from depth camera).

Degeneracy arises in the essential matrix/rotation & translation matrix when estimating with one plane. It makes sense that when lacking an additional reference/depth information, guessing 3D motion reliably is not really possible, but it can be improved. Degeneracy due to a single plane which doesn't allow for depth information is better explained here: http://www.r-5.org/files/books/computers/algo-list/image-processing/vision/Richard_Hartley_Andrew_Zisserman-Multiple_View_Geometry_in_Computer_Vision-EN.pdf in 11.9.2 where a more formal description is given that boils down to the fact no additional plane doesn't constrain what skew-symmetric matrix can be constructed as an answer.

Some improvements already made is using the KLT feature tracker from cv2 instead of orb which forces points to be based on spatial locality, meaning they sre constrained from varying too much from frame to frame, instead of distinct pixelation contrast information, which seems to vary more due to intensity changes over light angles (causing jumps across frames). This leads to one of the issues found with trying the baseline essential matrix function from cv2 with points tracked wildly varying to be combatted a bit, with the trajectory now appearing more stable. Inspecting KLT_cv2.png compared to ORB_cv2.png, it becames clear the initial path remains straight for longer (with ORB initially doing more twists as evidenced by the thicker line), less spinning/loops are committed overall, and the length of the trajectory is farhter in the initial direction of travel, which shows it's getting the net motion more accurately to some degree. This may change for frames with more pronounced objects to track. The loss of the initial straight path could possibly be signaling the increasing numerical error and inherent drift in the estimate from taylor series applicaiton?
